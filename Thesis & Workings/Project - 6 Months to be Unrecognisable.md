
##### Part One: Identity Overhaul 

![Pasted image 20260317151932.png](app://f1c4edd1323c1ca33c31cecc571af31bdbd5/Users/Winona/Desktop/Anthropological%20Perspective%20on%20Pop%20Culture/Image%20Bin/Pasted%20image%2020260317151932.png?1773760772944)
**If you don't change you identity, you'll drag old problems into a new life.** Often, people fail to improve their life because they're simply stapling new habits over the top of old ones, dragging the old unhelpful aspects of their life along with them. This, of course, requires energy and effort to keep moving *despite* something, rather than untethering yourself from the old behaviour to preserve energy to build the new ones. It's like trying to build a city on a swamp, and that leaves you with Florida, which nobody really wants. Real change starts by either working to make the land viable to build on, or simply finding a new patch of land and leaving the swamp behind. You behave in a way that matches the story you tell yourself, even if that story is outdated. If you believe you're a yoyo dieter who can never stay in shape, you doom yourself to repeating the cycle. 

Everything from how you eat, dress, act, talk, sit in a chair has to align with the new version of yourself. Everything that does not align has to be evaluated and either brought into alignment, or abandoned and left with the other dead weight. If you don't want to be someone who sits around watching TikToks all day, stop watching TikToks all day. If you want to be more intelligent, read and learn something new. If there's a mismatch between who you say you are and the way you behave, it creates a cognitive dissonance, a loss of identity, and a detachment from the self. That can lead to feelings of inauthenticity, which is portrayed as uncertainness or self-consciousness, which are tangible to other people (see [[Being Hot is a Mindset]]). You need to start to align yourself with the aspirational self as efficiently as possible.

Stop thinking of yourself as a product of your past and start thinking of yourself as a product of your decisions. Every choice you make works to chip away at or solidify your idealised self. It's encouraged to do some soul seeking and some [[Shadow Work]] to discover and embrace the features of yourself that you deem to be negative. It's okay to do things that are out of character. Perfection is not a character trait by any means. This action is more like casting votes; you don't have to do one huge election and cast every ballot at once, just vote strategically to remove power and agency from the parts of you that you want to remove (again, this can only truly be done through [[Shadow Work]] and through loving the hedonistic, perverse, otherwise 'sinful' elements of your own nature). It's important to begin auditing your life, that means taking inventory of your habits, actions, and beliefs, and questioning whether these things serve the future you're working to build. This is an area in which you can't afford to be gentle - stop settling for 'it's fine', because that's not good enough. 

You can't think your way into a new life. Imagining it will not make it real. Waiting for the right moment is how you lose years of your life. Speak the way you want to speak, dress the way you want to dress, do the things that this aspirational version of yourself would do, and do them badly at first. There is no way to build a new life without laying the bricks. You need to treat this identity shift as a religion, in the sense that it requires an enormous amount of faith and belief, rituals, and practical applications of teachings. Make the identity sacred. Your old self will not want to die quietly, you will be drawn into the temptations of comfortability often and with an enormous fire. It's okay to falter every now and again, in fact I would argue it's normal and part of the journey as a whole. If anything, it can be beneficial to go back to comfort so long as you reflect on how it makes you feel. Those negative emotions - the disappointment, the frustration with yourself - can only act as fuel for your own fire. Eventually, this will burn away the older, more restrictive habits until they come behavioural outliers rather than your normal. 

When you begin to accept authority and agency in your own life, taking accountability for all decisions (both 'good' and 'bad'), you stop waiting for permission to change. This helps to create the most authentic and well-rounded version of yourself, because it is one build entirely for you, by you, from the foundation up. The versions of ourselves that desire change but cannot reach it are limited by being a collection of various parts, assembled from the ideals and expectations of others. This, again, causes a detachment from the self - how many of our habits feel predetermined? Do you feel like you can't start running because you don't have the physical fitness to cover a good distance? Your expectations of what 'a good distance' means have been set by external sources rather than measured against your own experience, and this makes it daunting. There is nothing scary about trying something new and 'failure' is not a bad thing. It is how we set a new standard, a new starting point. We don't need to compare our goals to the achievements of others when the goal is simply to be better than the day before. This is what drives growth. When we can set expectations untethered to the bragging rights of those vastly more experienced than us, or from our peers, or from an influencer, the focus simply becomes on being better than the version of you that existed last time you tried to do this specific task. It creates a realistic marker, a more emotionally tangible measure of progress, and removes the shame of inability. 

So, who am I going to become?

In six months time, I will be an esoteric pilates doll. I have a deep breadth of knowledge, and I communicate in an intelligent way. I only consume content that either sparks joy or furthers my pursuit of knowledge. I am fit and healthy, strong and slim, I go to the gym whenever I can to take care of my body and give me time to think. Going to the gym clears my mind and helps me get the most restful sleep possible, sweating feels like letting go of anxiety and stress. I eat well, cycling through set meals that nourish my body and align with my health goals. I don't eat ultra processed foods, nor am I tempted by them. I eat a variety of whole foods, with ferments for my gut health, fibre to keep me satiated, and plenty of vegetables for vitamins. I eat red meat weekly. I spend my spare time reading and writing, always extending my knowledge and honing my craft of the written word.  I paint, draw, sew - anything to stretch my creative muscles, and anything that keeps me from doom-scrolling endlessly. I read the newspaper, but never buy the same one twice. I am educated on politics, philosophy, science, art, fashion, and literature. I enjoy talking about these things, but prefer listening to others talk about the areas where my own knowledge is less broad. Every conversation is an opportunity to learn. I have a variety of [[Things I Find Chic]] and stick to them. I am a fully realised person. I always look put together, and yet am an individual. I do not follow trends or seek constant inspiration/validation for my choices - I follow my own intuition and have the self-belief and self-love to trust myself. I am calm and collected, in tune with myself and with the universe. I meditate and practise yoga to further my connections. My life is a blessing, and I am grateful for it. Both the 'good' and the 'bad' in my life are there for a reason. I know there is no joy without sadness, no light without dark, and I am thankful for each of these in their own way. I understand the hermetic treadmill, I understand my own spirituality and faith, my own emotions and logic, my own internal duality. I am grounded, yet still capable of aspiration. I want to grow, and every day I will try to be better than I was the day before. I am happy at work. I am grateful for these opportunities, even when they test me because this is the life I used to dream of, and these are the opportunities I once was begging for. I am unlimited and curated and spiritual and scientific and ego-less and egotistical all at once. I am infinite in nature, because I was made in the image of infinity itself. This is who I am. 

How do I get there?

1. **Daily voluntary discomfort.** Increase the amount of time you spend uncomfortable weekly, or daily if possible, to encourage growth. Over time, the baseline for discomfort or for what you consider to be uncomfortable will shift creating a new internal standard and raising self esteem. This includes adding friction to tasks (eg. writing independently from source material or cooking without a recipe) to build intuition and repair neural pathways. Practice meditation often and relish in silence and boredom to shift the baseline for dopamine production and enjoyment. Note when certain tasks no longer feel uncomfortable. 
2. **Induce an abundant mindset by expecting miracles.** Focus and note any amazing or abundant thing through journalling and reflecting on this. Note feelings associated with the little things in life that just seem to go your way. Affirm these as they occur eg. thinking 'everything is always working out for me'. When something does go wrong, rationalise how and why it happened, and try to reframe it as something wonderful. Our fabric of reality is malleable!
3. **Complete a project.** Close a loop, pick something you've been stalling on and get it finished. It doesn't have to be perfect. Do what you can to finish whatever you start wherever feasible, and reflect on your ability to get shit done. You are someone who is capable of finishing things!
4. **Action bias.** Follow the plan. You are an avatar to be moved and guided through actions. It doesn't matter how you feel about a task, you can still do it. You have agency over your own body, and when you do something despite not really feeling like it, you teach yourself that you are resilient and disciplined, that you love yourself enough to show up and take care of yourself. Practice physiological sighs (two quick inhales and a long sigh) to regulate the nervous system. Follow Wim Hof breathing exercises for meditation and explore breath work. 

**It is never too late to take agency and change your life.**

I've also made this Venn diagram, showing the difference in the steps I take in a normal daily routine vs my idea of a perfect or aspirational day. The disparity between the two is made to seem larger due to slight differences in actions I take vs actions my aspirational self would take, for example adding steps to my skincare routine, not having biscuits with my tea/coffee when I arrive at the office, using free time to journal or study rather than scroll online. This exercise helped me realise that there actually aren't too many changes necessary to achieve what I consider to be a 'perfect' day, and that most of the things I need to change to do with idling away time. I tend to spend a significant portion of my day using social media purely to browse, not really gaining any inspiration or using it to contact people and form connections. I just sort of stare into the void and hope for the best. The main thing I can do to move in the right direction to achieve my aspirations is simply to better manage how I use my free time. This will also move me closer to that version of myself, improving my intelligence, literacy, and vocabulary, as well as my attention span. Limiting my time on social media will also be good for my self doubt, and this obsessive comparison issue I seem to have developed. 

That may seem like a really obvious observation - that doing the things I want to do will bring me closer to being the person I want to be. I think I have spent far too long trying to become a whole new person all in one go, rather than chipping away at it a bit at a time. By making small changes to my day, I can start moving in the right direction. It all feels a bit like swimming upstream, but the only salmon that move with the current are the dead.
