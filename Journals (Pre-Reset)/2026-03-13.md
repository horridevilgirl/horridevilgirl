Lately I’ve been trying to articulate a feeling that has followed me for most of my life: the sense that I’m secondary to my own existence. Like I’m standing behind a thick pane of glass, watching myself move through life on autopilot while another version of me, an internal observer, judges every move I make.

This observer feels like the superior one. She’s intelligent, composed, capable, creative - the person I imagine I _should_ be. Meanwhile the version of me actually living life, the subject, feels like a disappointing child constantly trying to impress her. The problem is that the observer keeps moving the goalposts. By the time I get close to meeting one standard, she has already raised it, and approval never arrives.

I’ve realised this dynamic has turned my ambition into something poisonous. I thought ambition meant wanting to grow, but what I’ve actually been doing is tying my worth to becoming extraordinary. Life has felt like a permanent audition. If I can’t perform at the level of my imagined self, I hesitate to perform at all.

A big part of the paralysis comes from living in vision rather than process. I can picture my “realised self” very clearly; someone intentional, creative, socially confident, living a full and meaningful life. But I struggle to begin because the small steps that lead there feel trivial or embarrassing compared to the vision. Instead of accepting that progress is made of messy, unremarkable increments, I’ve been waiting for steps that feel worthy of the destination.

Social media has made this worse. It constantly feeds the observer images of people my age who appear to already be living the life I want. The result is an endless cycle of comparison: inspiration turning into envy, then discouragement, then more scrolling. It keeps me in observer mode. I'm just watching other people live instead of participating myself.

Another realisation is that I think _too much about thinking_. I’m able to analyse my behaviour, name my patterns, and explain why I’m stuck, but insight alone doesn’t create change. The part of the brain that understands problems is not the same part that initiates action. So I’ve been pacing around the metaphorical swimming pool discussing swimming techniques instead of getting in the water.

Underneath everything sits another layer of fear: if I truly try, if I write, create, share ideas, then my potential becomes testable. I might discover that I’m not as talented or exceptional as I imagined. Strangely, it has felt safer to remain in a state of “almost,” where my potential remains intact but unused.

The real shift I need isn’t to kill ambition or silence the observer entirely. The observer has good instincts; taste, curiosity, standards. But she shouldn't remain the manager. Right now the relationship is Judge and Performer. It needs to become Mentor and Apprentice.

The part of me that actually creates, experiments, speaks, and lives is the imperfect subject. She’s messy and inconsistent, but she’s also the only part capable of producing anything real. The observer can advise, but she cannot create. What I’m beginning to understand is that a meaningful life isn’t built through flawless performances or dramatic leaps toward an ideal self. It’s built through small, imperfect actions repeated over time, through curiosity, experimentation, and tolerance for mediocrity along the way.

My task isn’t to become the realised version of myself overnight. My task is simply to become someone who participates in her own life again.
