People are smoking again. The data says this isn't true - less people are smoking. But I still see it all the time. There's a link between drinking and smoking, or plenty of people who don't consider themself anything more than 'social smokers'. Cigarettes, without any real intentional marketing aside from scary pictures of tumours and men with erectile dysfunction, have retained their status as an accessory to whatever is deemed 'cool' right now - Bushwick Line cooks, Blue Collar Cosplayers, girls with a chic French aura. I've noticed a lot of people recently calling out performative smoking; as in the act of smoking as something you do to seem cool rather than something you do because you just really love cigarettes. People who fall into the latter camp will sometimes take it upon themselves to comment online on the smoking habits of the performative nicotine addicts in the former camp, making sure that everyone knows that they themselves are gold star nicotine addicts who are addicted to a substance for their own enjoyment rather than being addicted to a substance because it says something about them. The big irony here is that needing people to know that you smoke in an authentic way rather than smoking as an accessory is performative as fuck. Smoking is a really costly performance, both financially and in terms of your health. So why would anyone put on this performance? What does it add to your life? Social credentials? Shorthand for taste? *Aura?*

It could be argued that a lesser form of 'performative-ness' is reading on public transport. This is a recognised form of 'aura farming' amongst a certain demographic of men. The way that the word 'aura' has entered our collective vernacular language interests me greatly. It's something that's always existed, in whatever form or stupid label we've applied to it (think rizz, swag, dopeness, freshness etc etc) - it's just the concept of being cool, attractive, and socially/sexually advanced. But aura points has given us a new capitalistic system with which to measure how well you can adhere to a set of arbitrary rules; a measurement system with theoretical units that can tell you where you are on the social ladder. This what sets aura apart from its predecessors, and in turn, is what makes it more addictive than any other form of in-ness that has come before it. 

Smoking cigarettes? Aura. 

This is one of the many, many, *many* problems faced by a generation of young people who are desperate to fit in, slaves to grind culture, and total consumerists. All of these elements feed into one another like a great cultural Ouroboros, each one consuming the last in order to strengthen its hold. Work harder to buy more to be on trend to help you make a good impression and get promoted to buy better, newer, trendier things which you can post about online and maybe get a brand deal. On and on and on and on. Everything is addictive, and everyone is addicted in their own way which to them, feels authentic and anyone doing this same thing any other way is performative. They're just trying to aura farm, but goated HTN chads like us are smoking outside Bershka in our cropped boxy hoodies because it's who we really are. 

You can also go into aura debt, and it can take years to pay it all off. If you are publicly embarrassed in, say, a street interview setting, the interviewer can effectively rob the interviewee of his or her aura, whilst simultaneously increasing his or her own. The interviewee, now freshly disgraced within a niche online community that they are a part of (without them being a part of the community, this transference of aura cannot take place. Aura acquisition and/or loss requires all individuals participating in the transaction to care about the thoughts and opinions of an active audience), the aura-less interviewee must work to rebuild their aura credit, either through proving their worthiness via a slow drip of aura-affirming content consumed by the same captive audience they were disgraced in front of, or by simply disgracing another in order to have his or her aura transferred over to themself. These are (in simplified terms) the methods of acquiring aura. 

The important thing to understand is that preceding aura is *attention*. If you do a cool or stupid or outlandish thing but nobody is there to see it or pay attention to it in a meaningful way, you can't really gain or lose aura. Unless, of course, it's recorded and posted online by someone with an audience who would care for that sort of thing, but then unless you manage to find that clip, and unless a member of that community comes across your account and draws your attention to your devastating aura loss/monumental aura gain, it doesn't (or shouldn't) really affect you. Aura is a part of you, and comes down to your own perception of yourself. In a way, it's kind of tangible. (see [[Shadow Work]] )

Attention is also a currency. We **pay** attention. You can be in **deficit** of attention, chronically so. We all want attention, to some extent. Attention is a prerequisite for love, support, community, praise, correction. Almost anything anyone can do for or to you requires them to pay you attention. Giving your attention to someone or something shows you care. You begin to take on traits of the people and things you pay attention to, forming this internal community in your mind. An interconnected family tree of things and people that mean something to you, things that are **worth** your attention. Before the advent of modern technological creature comforts like laptops and Instagram Reels, our attention was reserved for friends, family, community leaders, books, hobbies, pets. Now our attention has to also cover things like laptops and Instagram Reels, so we either have to find a way to get more attention to spend, or pay less attention to more things. This, in a capitalistic model, drives up the metaphorical cost of our attention spans. And there is nobody who wants your attention quite as badly as advertisers. 

It's valuable because even if you're flat broke, unable to spend capital on material goods, you still have mental real estate up for grabs; you can still talk about this cute thing you saw, share about how these things can be associated with a certain type of person or aspirational lifestyle. You can still take the time to comment how much you want the thing in the advert or how stupid the thing is, driving engagement and pushing it further out on your favoured social media platform, increasing the chance that someone with financial capital will spend some of their attention currency looking at the same advert and decide to buy. Your mental real estate is also valuable in the sense that you may start to save your financial capital to get the thing in the advert, putting it into that all important cultural self-ingesting mythological snake model discussed earlier. Advertisers will do whatever they can to get you to spread your attention thinner - to care less about your friends and your families and your hobbies and interests, to keep scrolling and stay engaged. Platforms are built to keep you there, because the advertisers pay them to show you more and more and more, to show you people who have the things you want, and who tell you what things you should buy to be the person you want to be. The snake eats itself and regurgitates itself to eat again. The cycle continues.
