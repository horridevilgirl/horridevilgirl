

Are you having a Brat Summer or a Bourdain Summer? Which trinket is hanging off your city bag? Where’s your Rhode lipgloss? I’ll see you at London Fields later, yeah? Cultural Stockholm Syndrome, Identity, and Self-Expression in the Age of the Internet. 

  

One minute you’re posting clips of you foraging figs on your Close Friends and preaching about slow mornings to the void of TikTok For You Page. The next you’re wearing an MSGM mini dress and spiralling in the disabled toilet because your friend’s friend who works at the cool pub in Islington said you remind them of their sister and you can’t find your vape. Somewhere between your city bag keychains and your Pinterest board of blurry world’s-best-ex-girlfriend photos, the question lands heavy: who even am I anymore?

  

And more pressingly: do I like this, or is it just the algorithm?

  

There’s a quiet, maddening pressure simmering under all of it; the need to have a look, a take, an identity scaffolded out of trend cycles. You don’t just dress yourself in the morning. You decide whether to be a half-French muse in archival Ann Demeulemeester, ordering espresso over ice in knee-high boots and asking your hinge matches for voice notes to see if they can pronounce 'Comme des Garçons' correctly, or a faux-Bourdain, swanning around farmers markets in fisherman sandals, posting something deeply profound about sardines on your food relics Substack and carting home approximately one tenth of your rent in olive oil and obscurely shaped Rummo pasta. Or maybe you’ve bypassed both and gone full-body linen and mushy-pea green. A druid in the city wondering whether your new found obsession with hand-made lace is a recession indicator or you’re just into it because Bella Hadid wore it to get a hoagie in Bushwick.

  

But here’s the problem: none of it sticks. The internet demands we pick a costume and wear it to death, yet punishes us for committing too hard. Aesthetic allegiance is seasonal now. Brat Summer ends the second Emma Chamberlain trades her Urban corset for a vintage Marlboro tee and three Saint James tops. Suddenly you’re dated. Suddenly you’re cringe. Suddenly you’re buying slug balm and telling people you're “experimenting with minimalism, actually, yeah”.

  

The identity crisis isn’t personal. It’s systemic. When culture flattens everything into content, from grief to breakfast to your sock choices, your selfhood becomes collateral. The self becomes a feed, then a brand, then an un-performable archive of choices made for an audience that doesn’t even exist. We try to root ourselves in trends because trends are legible. They let us opt in, signal tribe, buy coherence in 3–5 business days for European shipping. But trends don’t love us back. They move faster than we can metabolise.

  

Even your contradictions are pre-digested now. You’re allowed to be chaotic. In fact, you’re encouraged to be contradictory… as long as it’s curated. As long as it’s pinned. As long as it performs. Crying in a $600 hoodie. Posting poetry screenshots edited overtop fit-pics on the main story. Another iMessage-documented Menty-B sandwiched between Balenciaga flagship store dressing-room selfies. A follow up image of a large Selfridges yellow paper bag, captioned ‘afternoon sweet treat’, ironic usage of the Rio de Janeiro filter in full force. Calling it “nuance.” Calling it “girlhood.” Calling it an “era.”

  

This is the tyranny of aesthetics: they offer the illusion of control in a collapsing world. What you wear becomes a political act, or a coping mechanism, or a form of self-hypnosis. You’re not just building outfits, you’re building a self that feels liveable. The Rhode honey bun lip gloss is your shell, it means you’re taking care of yourself, unbothered, protecting your peace. The sardine tin candle is a sigil, it means you’re quirky and creative. The Monchhichi swinging on your city bag is a prayer that someone, anyone will see you and understand what you’re trying to say.

  

But what are you trying to say?

  

That you’re cool? That you’re coping? That you’re in on the joke?

  

There’s something tragically human in all of it. We’ve been trained to speak in visuals, to treat personal style as semiotic warfare, to outsource our tastes to curated spreadsheets and TikTok girlies who apparently do know what they’re doing. The Fleabag monologue haunts all of us: I want someone to tell me what to wear, what to eat, what to like, what to do. And honestly? Yeah, I do. Except it’s not a sexy priest telling me what to do, it’s an endless algorithm trained to sell me endless tat until my mind melts into pile of goo and I’m capable of little more than looking into the endless yawning maw of Zara dot com and filling my cart with fitted tees because I’m back ‘in my minimalist era’ thanks to a recently purchased tube of unscented Aesop hand cream.

  

And for a lot of us, especially women, it’s not just about taste - it’s about obedience. Girlhood is no longer a phase, it’s a reference point. A condition. A marketing strategy. Rather than just revisiting your childhood, you’re trying to make sense of it. We get nostalgic for listening to the teacher. We want to come downstairs and see that mum has laid out an outfit, socks and all. We yearn to submit to a set of rules. Now the all-knowing voice belongs not to someone who loves us, who cares about shaping our future, but to an algorithm that doesn’t give a shit about your wellbeing so long as it can show you TikTok’s “It Girl” routines and Instagram’s capsule wardrobe reels. The new school uniform is dead-stock white tees and selvedge denim. It’s a Heaven (by Marc Jacobs) baby tee and a Cowboys of Habit micro-mini. Everyone has the same Paloma Wool hobo bag. Your little sister is spending fifty quid on limited edition Starface pimple patches. We worship the idea of getting it “right”, of looking pulled together, palatable, approved. Minimalism isn't just an aesthetic; it’s a signal that you’re calm, competent, clean. Obedient. Contained. That you like dirty martinis and probably model. Maximalism says that you’re fun, prone to a pinger, you listen to Drain Gang and would love an eyebrow piercing if you weren’t so scared of it rejecting. You’re outgoing. Creative. Interesting.

  

Then, there are trinkets. Sylvanian Families on your shelf. Hello Kitty keychains on your Miu Miu bag. A Pop Mart doll in your hallway. I have the one and the only 24k gold Labubu. That mellifluous incantation has been ringing in my head all week, alongside Lizzo’s bar, ‘can’t even out-dress my Labubu’ (a particularly cutting insult from her new song “Yitty on Yo Titties”). We’re clinging to the same objects we used to project onto as kids, and new ones that have their own designer wardrobes; tiny tokens of imagined control. But the nostalgia runs deeper than aesthetics. It’s about power. At school, power was visual and domestic. Were you the mum in pretend play, or the dog? Who got to hold the baby doll? Who was called “the pretty one” at break time? Social status was dished out by size, looks, and accessories: Lelli Kelly shoes, braided holiday hair, the shiny surface of your Smiggle pencil case. The hierarchy was set early, and it was visible.

  

Now, we replicate the same logic just with different variables. You’re a Strawberry Girl. A Pilates Princess. A Tomato Girl. An Insta Baddie. Roles with prepackaged rules and designated aesthetics. It’s comforting, in a way, to climb inside a trope. To be told what to wear, what to eat, how to self-style into a person worth liking. These roles give us boundaries, relief from decision-making, and crucially a sense of belonging. Post-COVID, after two years of suspended selfhood, we crave shared language. The internet gives us that, but it often speaks in cliches. And if you squint, every niche aesthetic is just a new lunch table. The adults are gone. We’re assigning each other new roles in the playground. I can see that you like what I like. You’re safe.

  

But aesthetics are only powerful inside the rooms that know how to read them. My Tabis are social currency in East London, but when I visit family in Liverpool, they’re just “the camel toe shoes”. My colleagues hate them. My friends are obsessed. Context is everything. Subcultural status symbols don’t translate outside the algorithmic microclimate, and even inside it, they mutate into something colder, more competitive. Every community that claims to be about individualism ends up chasing the same grails, quoting the same niche theorists, pinning the same Dion Lee top from 2012. We’re all completely individual, and totally the same. Our desire to be distinct unites us and we spiral into a competition for the most obscure reference, but we’re looking at the same materials because we don’t know how to find anything that isn’t handed to us along with a convenient advert for up to 60% off on Vestiare. It really is ironic: the more niche we become, the more interchangeable we look. We build ourselves out of rare pieces, but all source from the same curated lists. Even “supporting small” becomes a flex that’s only valid if the brand’s had a Dazed feature or a campaign shot on expired film. We claim to reject the mainstream, but we still need external proof of taste. This isn’t community. It’s consumption all-dressed-up as something genuine. It’s the illusion of belonging, held together by gate-kept knowledge and mutual paranoia. We mirror each other’s habits, buy each other’s favourites, speak in shared references, and we still feel alone. 

  

Because none of it escapes capitalism. The algorithm doesn’t care how rare your taste is. It’ll find a way to sell it back to you. The machine is smarter than you, faster than you, and infinitely more committed to the bit. No matter how obscure your interest, it can be monetised, or worse, turned into a trend. If you think you’re immune, you’re wrong. Capitalism doesn’t just want your money. It wants your identity. And then it wants to sell you the feeling of finally having one again, and again, and again.

  

Individuality has become a performance so over-rehearsed it’s practically theatre in the round. We all know the lines. “I don’t follow trends.” “I like things that are weird.” “I’ve always felt a bit out of place.” But now that everyone is an outsider, now that the algorithm feeds us obscure Eastern European indie bands alongside vintage Coperni campaigns and 90s McQueen runway clips, being niche is basically the new mainstream. And in an attention economy hooked on artificial newness, the most radical thing you can do might be to embrace the average. Deliberately.

  

A few months ago, before I deleted social media (which is a trend within itself, and if that doesn’t prove my point then I’m not sure what will), I came across a reel of Jonathan Leandoer (aka Yung Lean, my patron saint of post-genre and figure of unexpected creative longevity) saying something that stuck itself in the visceral meat-heap of my content-fatigued brain. He said: “The party is officially over. It’s time to make it cool again to do simple shit.”

  

Here’s someone who basically built the blueprint for alternative internet fame from defining the early 2010s cloud rap sound with Drain Gang and Sad Boys, to shape-shifting through industrial (Warlord), ambient (Starz), singer-songwriter melancholy (Jonathan), to collaborations with everyone from Dean Blunt and Frederik Valentin to Travis Scott. He’s made a career out of pushing aesthetic boundaries and curating a mythos around himself. And now, he’s choosing stillness. Simplicity. He’s romanticising the mundane.

  

It feels oddly transgressive.

  

Because maybe we’re all tired of the curation. Maybe resisting the algorithm doesn’t look like logging off, or trying to be even more obscure, maybe it’s just sitting still. Wearing the same jumper three days in a row. Not posting it. Romanticising the Tesco meal deal. Leaning into the beige, the boring, the blissfully average.

  

What if the new flex isn’t edge, but ease?

  

In a culture where every detail of your life is content; your coffee order, your outfit, your sadness, choosing not to document is practically a radical move. Choosing not to reinvent yourself every season might be the only thing that actually feels real anymore.

  

Buying new things doesn’t make me feel good. It makes me feel like a cheap hack and a cog and, if I’m being really honest, a total fucking loser. We don’t just buy clothes anymore. We buy the version of yourself who might feel good in them. We buy the calm, the control, the cohesion. We buy the fantasy of being un-conflicted. We pay to be one thing.

  

I don’t want to pay to be put in a box. I don’t want to work to buy things so that people know I read Russian literature and listen to ambient. I don’t want to live under the pressure of the nonexistent voyeur-slash-fashion-critique. I just want to be a person.

  

Real people are messy. Conflicted. Contradictory. You’re not a moodboard. You’re not a product. And you’re definitely not a trend.

  

So maybe the challenge isn’t to escape the system, because we can’t, but to learn how to move inside it with a bit of grace. A bit of humour. A bit of bite. Be self-aware, sure. Be well-dressed. But don’t let the outfit do all the talking. You’re allowed to like the trinkets. You’re allowed to shift your aesthetic every season. Just don’t mistake the packaging for the point. The point is you.
