> This curriculum is built around thematic units rather than subjects, because your mind works in connections rather than categories. Each unit is anchored by a central question and approached through four lenses: **art & culture**, **philosophy & ethics**, **psychology & the self**, and **science & nature**. The units build on one another, complete them in order where possible, but return to any of them whenever life makes one feel relevant.

# Unit Zero: The Anthropological Imagination

_Why we do what we do, become who we become, and build the worlds we build_

> This unit sits before all the others because it is the foundation beneath them. Every other unit in this curriculum asks a specific question, about the body, beauty, identity, desire, the authored self. This unit asks the question underneath all of those questions: **what kind of creature is a human being, and why does it behave this way?**

---

## Central Question

Why do humans, across all cultures, all eras, all circumstances, consistently do the same things: build hierarchies, create idols, form tribes, perform rituals, tell myths, seek the sacred, make art, fall in love with beauty, and construct elaborate systems of meaning to make sense of a universe that offers none by default?

This is the anthropological question. It is also, quietly, the question your entire life is asking.

---

## Why This Unit Comes First

Everything else in this curriculum, the gaze, the shadow, the authored self, the body, makes more sense when you understand it as an expression of something deeper: the human animal's compulsive need to make meaning, build identity, and locate itself within a story larger than itself.

The "esoteric pilates princess" is not just an aesthetic. She is a **mythological figure**, an archetype, a cultural construction, a vessel for a particular set of human values and desires. Understanding _why_ humans need figures like this, why we create and inhabit and worship them, is the anthropological question. And answering it will make your understanding of your own self-construction richer and stranger and more interesting than any style guide could.

## The Thinkers Who Will Feel Like Home

**Joseph Campbell**, _The Hero with a Thousand Faces_ Campbell's central argument is that across every culture in human history, the same story gets told: a hero leaves the ordinary world, undergoes transformation through trial and encounter with the shadow, and returns changed. He calls this the **monomyth**, the single story underneath all stories. Read this and you will never watch a film, read a novel, or examine your own life the same way again. Campbell is also one of the most readable serious thinkers of the 20th century, begin here.

**Carl Jung**, _The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious_ (selected essays) You've already encountered Jung through shadow work. Go deeper. Jung's theory of archetypes, the universal figures and patterns that recur across all human cultures in myth, dream, and art, is the psychological foundation for Campbell's work and for most serious thinking about why humans create the symbols and idols they do. The archetypes are not invented by individuals. They arise from what Jung calls the **collective unconscious**, the shared psychic inheritance of the entire species.

**Mircea Eliade**, _The Sacred and the Profane_ Eliade was a historian of religion who argued that the distinction between the sacred and the ordinary is one of the most fundamental structures of human experience, that humans are irreducibly _homo religiosus_, meaning-seeking creatures who cannot help but locate certain times, places, objects, and people in a category of the sacred. Even in secular life, celebrity culture, fashion, music, sport, the sacred persists in new forms. This is essential reading for understanding why we create idols.

**Claude Lévi-Strauss**, _Myth and Meaning_ Lévi-Strauss argued that myths are not primitive attempts at science or history, they are the way human minds organise and process the fundamental contradictions of existence: life and death, nature and culture, self and other. _Myth and Meaning_ is short and accessible and will change the way you read everything.

**René Girard**, _Violence and the Sacred_ (selected sections) Girard's theory of **mimetic desire**, the idea that we don't know what to want until we see someone else wanting it, that desire is fundamentally imitative and social rather than individual and authentic, is one of the most unsettling and liberating ideas you will encounter. It reframes everything: fashion, celebrity, social media, relationships, the formation of taste. Why do we create idols? Because we need models for desire, and we call those models icons.

**Victor Turner**, _The Ritual Process_ Turner's concept of **liminality**, the threshold state between one identity and another, the in-between space of transformation, is directly relevant to everything your document is trying to do. Every rite of passage, every initiation, every moment of genuine change involves a liminal phase: the old self has dissolved but the new self has not yet solidified. You are in a liminal phase right now. Turner will help you understand what that means and why it is necessary.

**Mary Douglas**, _Purity and Danger_ Douglas argues that what any culture defines as dirty, taboo, or transgressive tells you everything about how that culture organises meaning and identity. The shadow self, the parts of yourself you've labelled deviant, shameful, or unacceptable, is a direct product of the cultural categories you've internalised. Douglas gives you the anthropological tools to examine those categories critically.

**Émile Durkheim**, _The Elementary Forms of Religious Life_ (selected sections) Durkheim, one of the founders of sociology, argued that religion is not primarily about God, it is about **community**. The sacred is a way of marking the things a group holds in common, and ritual is the technology by which communities renew their collective identity. Apply this to fashion communities, music subcultures, aesthetic tribes, and it becomes electric.

## The Central Themes

**Myth and the human need for story** Humans are the only animals that live inside narratives. We cannot experience our lives as raw events, we immediately convert them into stories with meanings, patterns, and arcs. Campbell and Jung argue this is not a cultural overlay but a biological necessity. The self is a story the brain tells itself. The curriculum is an act of rewriting that story.

**The idol, the icon, and the archetype** Why do humans worship? Not just gods, but celebrities, designers, musicians, cultural figures who seem to embody something larger than themselves. The answer is archetypal: we project onto certain figures the qualities we most need to see made real. Jane Birkin, Sofia Coppola, Frida Kahlo, these are not just women. They are **cultural archetypes**, vessels for a particular constellation of values that a particular community needs to see embodied. Understanding this doesn't diminish them. It makes them more interesting.

**Ritual and the technology of transformation** Every culture has technologies for changing who you are: initiation rites, pilgrimages, fasting, meditation, confession, therapy. These are not different things, they are the same thing in different cultural vocabularies. The practices in your document, the daily discomfort, the journalling, the meditation, the shadow work, are rituals in the anthropological sense. They work because ritual has always worked, because the human nervous system responds to repeated symbolic action in ways that pure cognition cannot replicate.

**Community, tribe, and the formation of identity through belonging** We do not form our identities alone. We form them in relation to groups, by identifying with some and distinguishing ourselves from others. Aesthetic communities (the people who wear Sandy Liang, the people who read Clarice Lispector, the people who do pilates and study philosophy) are tribes in the anthropological sense: groups organised around shared symbolic systems. Understanding this makes the desire for community, and the difficulty of finding it, make much more sense.

**The sacred in secular life** Eliade's insight that the sacred never disappears, it only changes form, is one of the most useful ideas for understanding contemporary culture. The gym as temple. The wardrobe as ritual. The reading practice as devotion. The aspiration toward a better self as a secular religion. These are not metaphors. They are structurally identical to their sacred predecessors, serving the same psychological and social functions.

## Self-Discovery Homework

_Unit Zero: The Anthropological Imagination_

**Map your personal mythology.**

Every person has a private mythology, a set of recurring symbols, figures, stories, and images that organise their inner life. This exercise is an attempt to make yours visible.

In your notebook, answer the following slowly and without overthinking:

- What stories (films, books, myths, fairy tales) have followed you through your life, the ones you return to, that feel personally significant beyond their content?
- What figures, real or fictional, living or historical, have you consistently been drawn to or wanted to be like? What do they have in common?
- What symbols, images, or objects feel charged or sacred to you, even irrationally?
- What recurring themes appear in the things you love, across music, film, art, fiction?
- If your life were a myth, what kind of myth would it be? What archetype are you living?

Then read what you've written and ask: **what is the story I am living inside, and is it the story I want to be living?**

## Writing Prompt

Write 700–900 words on this question: _What am I worshipping, and why?_

Not in a religious sense, in the anthropological sense. What do you treat as sacred? What rituals do you perform and what do they mean? What idols have you constructed, and what need do they serve? What myth are you living?

Write it honestly. Write it as an anthropologist studying the most interesting subject available to you, which is yourself.

## Unit One: What Is a Body?

_On physicality, embodiment, and the self that lives in skin_

**Central question:** Is your body something you _have_, or something you _are_?

This unit is the most personal and the most pressing. Your document names a difficult relationship with your physical self, this unit won't fix that, but it will give you a rich intellectual and creative framework for understanding it, which is the first step toward changing it.

**Art & Culture anchor** Start with Frida Kahlo, not her biography, but her paintings themselves, particularly _The Two Fridas_ and _My Birth_. Kahlo painted her body as both subject and object, both site of pain and site of power. As you look, ask: what does it mean to represent your own body rather than simply inhabiting it?

Alongside this, read John Berger's _Ways of Seeing_ (Chapter 3 in particular, on the female nude and the act of being watched). This is where your concept of the "internal voyeur" has its clearest intellectual mirror.

**Philosophy anchor** Read selections from Simone de Beauvoir's _The Second Sex_ (the introduction and chapter on "The Body"). De Beauvoir argues that women are taught to experience their bodies as objects to be seen rather than subjects that act. Her famous line, _"One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman"_, is the philosophical version of what your document is trying to do in practice.

Pair this with a short essay: Maurice Merleau-Ponty's concept of the _lived body_ (you can access summaries of his _Phenomenology of Perception_ without reading the whole text). His central idea is that the body is not a thing we inhabit, it _is_ our way of being in the world. This is the scientific and philosophical counter to the internal voyeur.

**Psychology anchor** Research the concept of **self-objectification theory** (Fredrickson & Roberts, 1997). This is the academic framework for exactly what you've named as the internal voyeur, the internalisation of an observer's perspective on your own body. Understanding the mechanism makes it easier to catch yourself doing it.

Also explore **interoception**, the scientific study of how we sense what's happening _inside_ our bodies (hunger, heartbeat, breath). Research suggests that poor interoceptive awareness is linked to body dissatisfaction. Practices like yoga, breath work, and the Wim Hof method you've already noted actively rebuild this awareness. You're already doing the science without knowing it.

**Science anchor** Read a summary of **Lisa Feldman Barrett's** work on embodied emotion (her book _How Emotions Are Made_ is accessible and fascinating). Barrett argues that emotions are not things that happen _to_ your body, your body actively _constructs_ them, based on past experience and present sensation. This reframes the relationship between feeling and physicality entirely.

**Writing prompt** Write 500–800 words in response to this question: _Whose eyes are you seeing yourself through, and when did you first borrow them?_ This is not an essay to be shared, it is an essay to be honest in. Use specific memories. Don't tidy it up.

## Unit Two: What Makes Something Beautiful?

_On aesthetics, taste, and the culture that shapes both_

**Central question:** Is your taste yours, or is it inherited?

This unit takes your home territory, art and culture, and subjects it to philosophical and scientific scrutiny. The goal is not to undermine your aesthetic sensibility but to _own_ it more fully by understanding where it comes from.

**Art & Culture anchor** Study the history of **aesthetics as a discipline**, begin with a short overview of how the idea of "taste" developed in 18th century Europe (particularly in the work of Kant and Hume). Then contrast this with **Susan Sontag's** _Against Interpretation_, her argument that we experience art too intellectually and not sensorially enough. Sontag is a natural home for someone with your sensibility.

Also spend time with the concept of **wabi-sabi**, the Japanese aesthetic philosophy that finds beauty in imperfection, impermanence, and incompleteness. Given your shadow self work, this philosophy has something specific to offer you.

**Philosophy anchor** Read Immanuel Kant's concept of the **sublime** (accessible summaries work well here), the idea that some experiences are beautiful precisely because they overwhelm us, because they exceed our ability to contain them. Then ask yourself: do you seek the sublime, or do you avoid it?

**Psychology anchor** Explore the psychology of **aesthetic experience**, particularly the research on _awe_ (Dacher Keltner's work is very readable). Awe is the emotion most associated with encounters with beauty and the sublime, and research suggests it is one of the most psychologically transformative emotions available to us, it literally makes the self feel smaller and less central, which is a relief.

**Science anchor** Look into the **neuroscience of beauty**, specifically Semir Zeki's research on what happens in the brain when we experience something as beautiful. The finding that aesthetic experience activates the same reward pathways as love and desire is worth sitting with.

**Writing prompt** Choose one thing you find beautiful, an object, an image, a piece of music, a moment. Write 500 words describing it without ever using the word "beautiful" or any of its synonyms. Then write a paragraph asking where your response to it came from.

## Unit Three: What Is the Self?

_On identity, authenticity, and the story we tell ourselves_

**Central question:** If you changed everything about yourself, would you still be you?

This is the philosophical core of your document, and arguably the oldest question in Western thought. This unit gives that question its full intellectual weight.

**Art & Culture anchor** Read **Roland Barthes'** short essay _The Death of the Author_, it argues that the meaning of a text is not fixed by the person who wrote it, but constructed by the person who reads it. Apply this to the self: are you the author of your identity, or is it being read and constructed by everyone around you?

Engage with **David Bowie** as a case study in performed identity, not just his music but his deliberate construction of personas (Ziggy Stardust, Aladdin Sane, the Thin White Duke). Was Bowie more authentic because he performed, or less? His interviews from the 1970s are extraordinary primary sources.

**Philosophy anchor** Read **Derek Parfit's** thought experiments on personal identity (from _Reasons and Persons_, Part III, summaries are fine). Parfit essentially argues that the self is not a fixed thing but a series of connected psychological states. This is both destabilising and deeply liberating, if there is no fixed self to protect, there is nothing to lose by changing.

Read also **Aristotle's** concept of _eudaimonia_, often translated as "happiness" but more accurately rendered as "flourishing" or "living in accordance with your nature." The question Aristotle asks is not _who are you?_ but _what are you for?_

**Psychology anchor** Study **narrative identity theory** (Dan McAdams), the idea that we construct our sense of self through the stories we tell about our lives. This connects directly to your document's observation about the story you tell yourself. McAdams' research suggests that the coherence and meaning of that narrative, not its content, is what determines psychological wellbeing.

Also explore **Carl Rogers'** concept of the **fully functioning person**, someone who is open to experience, lives in the present, trusts their own judgment, and is in a process of becoming rather than a finished state.

**Science anchor** Read about **neuroplasticity**, the brain's capacity to rewire itself in response to repeated behaviour and thought. This is the scientific foundation for everything your document describes. The habits you build are literally changing the structure of your brain. This is not metaphor. It is biology.

**Writing prompt** Write a short essay (600–900 words) titled _The Ship of Theseus, and Me_. The Ship of Theseus is a thought experiment: if you replace every plank of a ship one by one, is it still the same ship? Apply this directly and personally. What have you already replaced? What are you replacing now? What, if anything, do you want to keep?

## Unit Four: How Do We See and How Are We Seen?

_On perception, representation, and the gaze_

**Central question:** What is the difference between looking and seeing?

**Art & Culture anchor** Study **Laura Mulvey's** concept of the **male gaze** (her 1975 essay _Visual Pleasure and Narrative Cinema_, it is dense but worth it). Then trace how this concept has evolved and been contested. How does the gaze operate in the media you consume? How has it shaped what you think you should look like?

Visit a gallery, physically if possible, virtually if not, and spend time with portraits specifically. Notice whose gaze meets yours and whose doesn't. Notice what the painter seemed to want you to feel.

**Philosophy anchor** Read **Jean-Paul Sartre's** concept of _the look_ (from _Being and Nothingness_, Part Three, summaries work well here). Sartre argues that being seen by another person is a fundamentally transformative experience, it turns you from a subject into an object, at least momentarily. The internal voyeur you describe is essentially Sartre's look turned inward.

**Psychology anchor** Study **social comparison theory** (Leon Festinger) and its modern expression in social media research. The research is unambiguous: upward social comparison on image-based platforms reliably decreases self-esteem and body satisfaction. You already know this experientially. Understanding the mechanism may help make it feel less personal and more structural.

**Science anchor** Explore the science of **visual perception**, specifically how much of what we "see" is actually constructed by the brain rather than received from the world. Look into **change blindness** and **inattentional blindness**. The implication is radical: we never see reality directly. We see a model of reality built from expectation, memory, and attention.

**Writing prompt** Find a photograph of yourself that you dislike. Write 600 words from the perspective of someone who loves you, describing what they see in it. Then write a paragraph in your own voice reflecting on the gap between those two descriptions.

## Unit Five Point Five: The Shadow & The Pleasure Principle

_On hedonism, deviance, desire, and the self that refuses to be tamed_

**Central question:** What are you afraid to want, and what would happen if you stopped being afraid of it?

This unit sits between Unit Five and the rest of the curriculum deliberately, it is the dionysian counterweight to all the apollonian self-building happening elsewhere. It is also the academic and artistic extension of the shadow work you've already begun. Jung's shadow is not only the parts of yourself you suppress out of shame, it is also the parts that contain the most energy, the most vitality, and the most authentic desire. This unit is about meeting those parts with curiosity rather than judgment.

**A note on tone:** this unit is not about transgression for its own sake, nor about normalising harm. It is about the serious intellectual tradition that has always understood desire, deviance, and the refusal of social norms as sites of profound self-knowledge. The thinkers and artists here are not provocateurs. They are philosophers of the interior life.

**Art & Culture anchor** _Secretary_ (2002), begin here, since it is your reference point. Watch it again with fresh eyes: it is a film about a woman who discovers that what she wants is precisely what she has been told to be ashamed of, and who chooses to want it anyway. The radical act is not the desire, it is the acceptance. Watch it as a case study in shadow integration.

Alongside this, engage with the work of **Louise Bourgeois**, sculptor, surrealist, and one of the most unflinching artists of the interior life. Her _Maman_ series, her cell installations, and her journals are all extraordinary. Her work sits at the intersection of the body, desire, memory, and the shadow self.

**Philosophy anchor** Read **Georges Bataille**, specifically his short essay _Eroticism: Death and Sensuality_ (introduction only to start, it is dense but extraordinary). Bataille argues that eroticism, transgression, and the ecstatic are fundamental to human experience, not aberrations from it. His framework is one of the most serious philosophical treatments of desire ever written.

Also read **Friedrich Nietzsche** on the dionysian, specifically the relevant sections of _The Birth of Tragedy_. Nietzsche's argument that the suppression of the dionysian produces not virtue but neurosis is one of the intellectual foundations of everything your shadow work is doing.

**Psychology anchor** Return to **Jung's shadow** with more depth here, specifically his concept of the _integration_ of the shadow as the path to wholeness. The parts of yourself you listed in your shadow Venn diagram are not enemies to be defeated. They are, in Jung's framework, sources of enormous creative and vital energy waiting to be reclaimed.

Also explore **Esther Perel's** work on desire and the erotic, particularly her book _Mating in Captivity_. Perel argues that desire requires distance, mystery, and a self that is not entirely knowable, which connects directly to the "something strange and unknowable" quality you described wanting to embody.

**Science anchor** Read about the **neuroscience of pleasure and reward**, specifically the role of dopamine not as a pleasure chemical but as an _anticipation_ chemical. The research of Kent Berridge distinguishes between _wanting_ and _liking_, a distinction that has profound implications for understanding desire, addiction, and the difference between authentic pleasure and compulsive consumption.

Also explore research on **awe and self-transcendence**, moments when the self dissolves temporarily into something larger. This is the scientific face of the ecstatic experience Bataille and Nietzsche describe.

**Writing prompt** Write 500–800 words titled _In Defence of My Shadow._ Choose one attribute from your shadow Venn diagram, one you feel most ashamed of, and write the most generous, most intelligent defence of it you can. Not an excuse. A genuine argument for its value, its intelligence, its place in who you are. Write it as though you are defending it in front of someone who matters to you.

## Unit Six: The Authored Self

_On creative voice, a recognisable world, and the aesthetic as autobiography_

**Central question:** What would it mean for your point of view to be so fully realised that it was legible to others without explanation?

This unit is about the relationship between the interior life and the exterior world it generates, how artists, designers, filmmakers, and writers develop a visual and intellectual language so coherent and specific that their work becomes unmistakable. It is also, quietly, about you: about what your own authored self looks like when it is fully realised, and how you begin to build toward it consciously.

**Art & Culture anchor** Study three case studies in depth, not their biographies, but their _worlds_:

**Jane Birkin**, her style was never designed. It emerged from a philosophy: ease, sensuality, the refusal to try too hard as its own form of trying very hard. Study her not as a fashion icon but as a woman whose outer life was a direct and uncontrived expression of her inner one. What was she _actually_ like? What did she value? Read her interviews.

**Sofia Coppola**, arguably the most coherent visual author working in cinema today. Every film she has ever made takes place in the same world: beautiful interiors, feminine interiority, the melancholy of privilege, gauze and swimming pools and girls who are trapped in lovely things. Watch _The Virgin Suicides_, _Lost in Translation_, and _Somewhere_ back to back and ask: what is the consistent obsession? What question is she always asking? That recurring obsession is what a creative voice actually is.

**Sandy Liang and Chopova Lowena**, study these two labels as examples of authored visual language in fashion. Sandy Liang's world is hyper-specific: downtown New York girlhood, flea market femininity, softness with an edge, the particular romance of a certain kind of youth. Chopova Lowena draws from Bulgarian folk costume and hardware and creates something that could not belong to anyone else. Ask of both: what is the _point of view_? What do they love? What are they refusing?

**Philosophy anchor** Return to **Roland Barthes**, this time his essay _The Grain of the Voice_ alongside _The Death of the Author_. Barthes argues that what makes an artist truly distinctive is not technical skill but something more bodily and particular, a quality of presence that cannot be imitated because it is inseparable from the specific person producing it. This is the philosophical framework for everything this unit is about.

Also read **Susan Sontag's** journals (selections are available, her diaries from her twenties are extraordinary). Sontag was one of the most deliberate self-authors of the 20th century, she constructed herself consciously and recorded the process in real time. Her journals read like a masterclass in becoming.

**Psychology anchor** Explore the concept of **personal fable** in psychology, the tendency to experience one's own life as uniquely significant and narratively coherent. In adolescence this is considered a cognitive distortion; in the creative life, it is the engine of voice. The artist who has a strong creative identity has essentially done something productive with the personal fable, they have taken the sense of _I have a specific and unrepeatable perspective_ and built something from it.

Also revisit **narrative identity theory** (Dan McAdams, already in Unit Three) with this question specifically in mind: what is the narrative of your creative self? What are its recurring themes, its imagery, its obsessions? McAdams argues that the stories we tell about ourselves are not reflections of identity but its actual substance.

**Science anchor** Read about **pattern recognition and the default mode network**, specifically how the brain identifies signatures and consistencies across different examples of the same thing. This is the cognitive basis for why we recognise a Sandy Liang piece or a Coppola film, the brain has extracted a pattern from repeated exposure. The implication for your own creative voice is interesting: consistency over time is what creates recognisability, not a single grand statement.

**Writing prompt** Write 600–800 words answering this question: _If someone who had never met me encountered my creative work, my writing, my visual choices, my aesthetic decisions, what recurring obsessions, images, and questions would they find there?_ Write this as honestly and specifically as possible. Not what you want them to find, what is actually there, in the work you have already made and the things you are already drawn to. This is the beginning of a map of your creative voice.

## Unit Seven: How Do We Change?

_On transformation, habit, ritual, and becoming_

**Central question:** Is change something that happens to us, or something we do?

This unit is the synthesis, it brings everything together and returns it to the practical questions your document begins with.

**Art & Culture anchor** Study the concept of **metamorphosis** in art and mythology, Ovid's _Metamorphoses_ (selected stories), Kafka's _The Metamorphosis_, and transformation as a recurring motif across cultures. What does it mean that humans have always told stories about changing form?

**Philosophy anchor** Read **Simone Weil** on attention, her essay _Reflections on the Right Use of School Studies_ argues that the capacity to give full, patient, loving attention to something is the root of both intellectual and moral development. This connects your reading practice, your writing practice, and your inner life in one elegant idea.

Read also the **Stoics**, particularly Marcus Aurelius' _Meditations_ (any edition). The Stoics were essentially building a practice of identity change through daily reflection and ritual, 2,000 years before self-help existed.

**Psychology anchor** Study **habit formation research**, particularly BJ Fogg's _Tiny Habits_ and Charles Duhigg's _The Power of Habit_. The neuroscience of habit loops (cue, routine, reward) maps directly onto the action bias framework in your document.

Also revisit **Carl Jung's** concept of the **shadow**, since you're already doing shadow work, understanding its theoretical foundation will deepen the practice. Jung argued that integrating the shadow (rather than suppressing it) is the only path to wholeness. Sound familiar?

**Science anchor** Read about the **default mode network**, the brain system that is active when we're not focused on external tasks, associated with self-referential thought, mind-wandering, and rumination. Meditation and mindfulness practices demonstrably change the activity of this network. Again, you're already doing the science.

**Writing prompt** Write your own _Meditations_, in the style of Marcus Aurelius, write 10–15 short entries addressed to yourself, each one a reflection, a reminder, or a piece of counsel for the person you are becoming. Write them in second person (_you_, not _I_). Keep them honest. Keep them kind.

## Fiction Reading List

_Classics and modern classics, chosen to speak directly to your curriculum themes and aesthetic sensibility_

Fiction is not supplementary to the intellectual work, it is a different mode of the same inquiry. A novel can do things philosophy cannot: it puts you inside a consciousness, makes abstraction visceral, and lets you live inside a question rather than merely think about it. Read these alongside the curriculum units they connect to, or simply in the order that calls to you.

**Essential and immediate, start here**

- **The Bell Jar**, Sylvia Plath _(Unit One, Three)_ The interior life under pressure, the self observed and observing, feminine existence as suffocation and survival. Essential.
- **A Room of One's Own**, Virginia Woolf _(Unit Three, Five)_ Short, essayistic, urgent. Women, creativity, and the conditions required to have an intellectual life. Reads like a direct address.
- **The Waves**, Virginia Woolf _(Unit Three)_ Six selves rendered as pure interior monologue. The most ambitious novel of the self ever written. Difficult and transcendent.
- **Bluets**, Maggie Nelson _(all units)_ Written in numbered fragments, perfect for pocket reading. Philosophy, grief, colour, and desire woven into something entirely its own. A model for your notebook practice.

**Feminine interiority and the examined life**

- **Outline trilogy**, Rachel Cusk _(Unit Three, Four)_ Identity constructed entirely through conversation and observation. The self as negative space. Quietly revolutionary.
- **The Passion According to G.H.**, Clarice Lispector _(Unit Five Point Five)_ A woman has a confrontation with a cockroach and it becomes a mystical crisis of selfhood. Sounds absurd; is extraordinary. Lispector is the literary equivalent of your hedonism unit.
- **Wide Sargasso Sea**, Jean Rhys _(Unit Three, Four)_ The madwoman in the attic given her own voice. Colonisation of identity, the self destroyed by being seen through another's eyes.
- **The Awakening**, Kate Chopin _(Unit One, Three)_ A woman waking up to her own desires in 1899. Radical then, radical now. Short and devastating.
- **Convenience Store Woman**, Sayaka Murata _(Unit Three)_ A woman who simply refuses the social script entirely. Brief, strange, quietly radical.

**Desire, deviance, and the shadow**

- **Story of the Eye**, Georges Bataille _(Unit Five Point Five)_ Transgressive and intentionally extreme, approach when ready, not before. The literary companion to Bataille's philosophy. Not for shock value; for understanding the serious intellectual tradition around desire and transgression.
- **Lolita**, Vladimir Nabokov _(Unit Four, Five Point Five)_ One of the most important and most misread novels in the Western canon. Read it for the unreliable narrator, for what it shows about the gaze, for the way Nabokov makes you complicit. Approach critically.
- **The Virgin Suicides**, Jeffrey Eugenides _(Unit Four)_ Feminine mystery from the outside, the male gaze as narrator, the unknowable interior life of women rendered as mythology.
- **Fanny Hill**, John Cleland _(Unit Five Point Five)_ The first prose pornography in English, written in 1748. Worth reading as a historical and literary document of desire, female agency, and the body, more complicated and interesting than its reputation suggests.

**Transformation and becoming**

- **Metamorphosis**, Franz Kafka _(Unit Five)_ The original transformation narrative of modern literature. Read it as a study in identity, alienation, and the body that becomes unrecognisable.
- **Orlando**, Virginia Woolf _(Unit Three, Five)_ A person who lives for four centuries and changes sex halfway through. The most joyful and radical treatment of identity, time, and transformation in English literature.
- **The Secret History**, Donna Tartt _(Unit Five Point Five)_ Beauty, obsession, classicism, and the dionysian taken to its destructive extreme. Compulsively readable and genuinely philosophical.
- **Stoner**, John Williams _(Unit Five)_ A quiet novel about a man who loves literature and lives an unspectacular life with complete integrity. About what it means to dedicate yourself to learning. Will mean more to you once you're a few months into your own practice.

**The esoteric and the strange**

- **The Master and Margarita**, Mikhail Bulgakov _(Unit Five Point Five)_ The devil visits Soviet Moscow. Wickedly funny, philosophically rich, completely strange. One of the great novels of good, evil, and creative freedom.
- **Beloved**, Toni Morrison _(Unit One, Five)_ The body as site of trauma and reclamation. The self fractured and rebuilt. Morrison is essential reading, begin anywhere in her work.
- **Autobiography of Red**, Anne Carson _(Unit Three, Five Point Five)_ A retelling of a Greek myth as a contemporary novel in verse. Strange, beautiful, about what it means to be different and to desire anyway. Anne Carson's entire body of work belongs in your ecosystem.
- **The God of Small Things**, Arundhati Roy _(Unit Four, Five Point Five)_ Love, transgression, and the violence of social rules. Prose so beautiful it functions almost as poetry.

**Classics with direct curriculum relevance**

- **Madame Bovary**, Gustave Flaubert _(Unit Two, Three)_ A woman destroyed by the gap between the life she imagines and the life she has. Read it as a cautionary tale about consuming fantasy rather than building reality, and as the greatest novel ever written about aesthetics and desire.
- **Anna Karenina**, Leo Tolstoy _(Unit Three, Five)_ Identity, social performance, desire, and the self that refuses containment. Longer but deeply rewarding.
- **Mrs Dalloway**, Virginia Woolf _(Unit One, Three)_ One day, one woman, the entire interior life of a self. Short, dense, beautiful.
- **Middlemarch**, George Eliot _(Unit Three, Five)_ The greatest English novel about the gap between who we might have been and who we become. Long but one of the most rewarding reading experiences available.

## Self-Discovery Homework

_Experiments in living, one per unit, to be done alongside the reading_

These are not assignments. They are experiences designed to generate raw material, for your writing, your art, your self-knowledge, and your creative voice. They work by putting you in situations where you cannot think your way to an answer, only live your way there. Some will feel uncomfortable. That is the point.

Do one alongside each unit. Write about it in your notebook afterward, not a report, just whatever it left in you.

---

**Homework One, Go somewhere alone** _(Unit One: What Is a Body?)_ Go to a public place entirely alone, a gallery, a café, a park, a cinema, with no phone and no book. Sit for at least an hour. Notice how your body feels without a screen to mediate the experience. Notice how you occupy space when no one you know is watching. Notice what you think about when there is nothing to consume. This is a practise in physical presence and an experiment in solitude.

_Afterward, write: what did your body do when you stopped directing it?_

---

**Homework Two, Make something with your hands from nothing** _(Unit Two: What Makes Something Beautiful?)_ Cook a meal without a recipe, using only what you have. Or arrange flowers, or rearrange a room, or make a collage from torn paper. The medium is less important than the constraint: no instructions, no reference, no Pinterest. Trust your own aesthetic instincts entirely and make something purely from your own sense of what belongs together.

_Afterward, write: what choices did you make, and where did they come from?_

---

**Homework Three, Interview yourself** _(Unit Three: What Is the Self?)_ Set a timer for thirty minutes. Write answers to the following questions without stopping to think, write the first honest thing that arrives, not the considered or flattering thing:

- What do I actually believe, as opposed to what I think I should believe?
- What do I want that I've never told anyone?
- What am I most afraid people would think if they knew everything?
- What would I do if I knew no one would find out?
- What have I always known about myself that I've been pretending not to know?

Do not edit. Do not reread until the next day. Then read it as though it was written by someone else.

---

**Homework Four, Spend a day as the person you want to be** _(Unit Four: How Do We See and How Are We Seen?)_ Choose one ordinary day and live it entirely as your aspirational self, dress as she would, move as she would, make the choices she would make from morning to night. Not as performance for others, but as a private experiment. Notice where it feels natural and where it feels like a costume. Notice what assumptions other people make. Notice how you feel in your own body when you are living in alignment with who you want to be.

_Afterward, write: where was the gap between the performance and the feeling, and what does that gap tell you?_

---

**Homework Five, Do the thing you've been avoiding** _(Unit Five Point Five: The Shadow)_ Name one thing you have been avoiding out of shame, fear, or the belief that you are not the kind of person who does that. It can be small, a style of clothing you've wanted to wear, a conversation you've been putting off, a creative work you've been too afraid to start, an experience you've been curious about. Do it. Not dramatically. Quietly, privately, as an act of self-knowledge.

_Afterward, write: what did the avoidance cost you, and what did doing it give you?_

---

**Homework Six, Study someone you find magnetic** _(Unit Six: The Authored Self)_ Choose one person, living or not, known to you or not, whose presence or creative work you find genuinely magnetic. Spend an hour with their work, their interviews, their images. Take notes not on what they do but on _how they seem to be_, their quality of attention, the consistency of their obsessions, the way their interior life seems to organise their exterior one.

Then ask: what specific things does this person love? What do they refuse? What question does their life or work seem to be answering?

_Afterward, write: what does this tell me about what I find valuable, and therefore about my own creative voice?_

---

**Homework Seven, Write a letter from your future self** _(Unit Seven: How Do We Change?)_ Sit down and write a letter from the version of yourself that exists one year from now, the one who has done the reading, made the work, built the habits, and closed the gap between the present self and the aspirational one. Write it in detail. What does she know that you don't yet? What does she want you to stop being afraid of? What is she grateful that you started?

Seal it. Return to it in a year.

---

**Reading rhythm:** One anchor text per unit (full or selected). Supplement with essays and summaries. Aim for breadth over completion, it is better to read five things shallowly and return to two of them than to read one thing exhaustively and burn out.

**Writing rhythm:** One writing prompt per unit, plus free journalling as often as feels right. The prompts are not assignments, they are invitations. If a prompt doesn't move you, adapt it until it does.

**Noticing practice:** Keep a small notebook (physical, not digital) for observations. Things you find beautiful. Questions that won't leave you alone. Lines from things you've read. Moments where the internal voyeur appeared. This is your raw material.

**Audit rhythm:** At the end of each unit, return to your Venn diagrams. What has shifted? What has been confirmed? What new attributes belong on the diagram that weren't there before?
