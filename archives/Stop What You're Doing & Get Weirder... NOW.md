**Everywhere I look, I'm seeing weirdness reframed as something else - something more constrained, more *conservative*. In today's political climate, it's almost understandable; the need to reframe oddness through a lens of whimsy or of niche-ness or of intellect. Part of me finds joy in the fact that there is a growing desire for young women to be strange and unusual, but my inner sceptic that can simply not allow any kind of optimism to go unchecked has once again reared its ugly head and made some rather compelling points...**

Witchcraft, hedonistic tendencies, a macabre fascination with antique surgical instruments - all the markers of vital neural pathways forming in the frontal lobe of a twenty-something-year-old woman. There is once again a place on the internet for freaky, disgusting girls to share their freaky, disgusting interests. Upon entry level exploration of these quote un quote *weird* online communities, at least. When time is taken to break through the surface tension and head down through the depths, however, there is an almost invisible veil of conservatism that prevents one from breaking through into the twilight zone of true strangeness. It pushes back, keeping us in this purgatory of socially acceptable odd interests, the caveat for their acceptability usually being that the topic of interest is a whimsical or esoteric view of something that is centred around, deemed useful to, or otherwise beneficial to men. For example, the aforementioned interest in antique surgical instruments is a more whimsical (and therefore feminised) version of an interest in medicine, or science, which are seen as more masculine.
