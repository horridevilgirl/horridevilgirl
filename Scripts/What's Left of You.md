There's a concept in psychology called the "ought self". Tory Higgins came up with it in the late eighties as part of self-discrepancy theory, and it's basically the version of you that you feel obligated to be. Not even who you want to be, who you think you should be. And Higgins found that the gap between your actual self and your ought self is specifically associated with anxiety. Like, not general dissatisfaction. Anxiety. The chronic, low-level, something-is-wrong-and-I-can't-name-it kind of feeling that makes you feel like you're going to be sick.

If you've ever spent a Sunday afternoon feeling vaguely terrible for no identifiable reason, that might be the most validating sentence you've heard in a while.

And here's the part that I find a bit... I don't know, funny? Sad? Both?; the ideal self is almost entirely made of other people. She's assembled from everyone you've ever admired, or been intimidated by, or wanted to impress. She's not really you at all. She's a collage. She's a mood board. She's a very high-maintenance piece of collaborative fan fiction.

Okay so here's where it gets interesting to me. Because there's this whole tradition, in philosophy, in theology, in poetry, of approaching things by subtraction. Like, the idea that the way to really understand something isn't to keep adding description, it's to carefully take things away.

There's a theological concept called apophatic theology (or the via negativa) which is basically the practice of only describing God by what he isn't. Not: God is powerful and wise and omnipresent. But: God is not limited, not absent, not unjust. And the reasoning is that some things are so much larger than language that any positive claim you make about them will just shrink them. The only honest approach is to describe the shape of the space they occupy.

And separately, Keats, in a letter he wrote when he was like twenty-two, which is frankly annoying, described what he called negative capability. The ability to stay inside uncertainty and contradiction without, and I love this phrase, "an irritable reaching after fact and reason." Just sitting with not knowing. Letting something be unresolved without it needing to be fixed.

These are very different thinkers arriving at something similar from completely different directions, which I always think is a sign you're probably onto something real. The empty vessel. The beginner's mind. The deliberate practice of not-defining as the precondition for actually understanding anything.

And what took me a long time to understand is that she's not trying to help me become something. She's trying to stop me from resting inside whatever I already am. Because if I rest there, if I actually inhabit myself for a second without flinching, I don't need her anymore. If I don't act on my potential, it can't be disproved. My inaction and inability to produce or create or converse, will all be wasted potential rather than a lack thereof. 

There's research on this, on the psychological cost of contingent self-worth, which is basically when your sense of value as a person is tied to performance. Jennifer Crocker at Ohio State has done a lot of work on this and the findings are pretty bleak, unfortunately. People with highly contingent self-worth show higher rates of anxiety and depression, more fragile self-esteem, and crucially, less actual learning. Because when your worth is on the line you stop taking risks. You stop being curious. You perform competence instead of developing it.

Which is sort of the trap in full. You build the ideal self to feel more capable and in control. And she makes you worse at everything.

And the cruel irony is that the obvious solution, know yourself better, go deeper, journal more, meditate, do the work, is also kind of the trap. And I say this as someone who journals every day, meditates, does shadow work, makes vision boards. I am extremely, almost professionally, self-aware. And I think it does help, genuinely. But there's a point at which all that introspection starts to backfire. Because the more you make the self an object of attention, the more you reinforce what psychologists call the narrative self; the part of you that's always watching, always commentating, always taking notes. And to aspire to be better is to acknowledge that you are, currently, less. Which puts you on what's called the hedonic treadmill. This observed tendency we have to return to a stable baseline of happiness no matter what we achieve, because the goal just recalibrates. You want to be more intelligent, so you read and you learn, and then your baseline just becomes how intelligent you currently are, and the better version is still out of reach. Always just out of reach.

So the self-improvement project, taken too far, doesn't free you from the ideal self. It just gives her more material to work with.

But here's the thing I've actually noticed, and this felt like a little revelation when I clocked it, when I stop paying so much attention to what I am and give that attention to something external, something happens. Like, the architecture of the buildings on my walk to work. A piece of art in a gallery. A conversation I'm actually in. I turn into this other version of myself. I feel free from inhibitions and free from judgement, and I'm in touch with the emotions that the external thing is conjuring in me rather than the ones I'm supposed to be performing.

And it's not limited to the nice stuff either. Have you ever felt most yourself when you were really angry? Or been so genuinely sad that the thought of how red and snotty your face looks when you sob doesn't even cross your mind?

Experiencing anything without performance, even briefly, even accidentally, connects you to yourself as a subject rather than an object. And everything you learn about yourself in those moments is less like a fact and more like a taste.

I used to think that giving myself over to something else, to another person, to a piece of work, to a place, would somehow deplete me. Dilute me. Like attention was a finite resource and spending it outward meant less of it for the project of being myself. But actually the opposite is true. In the moments where I can fully, organically attune to something outside myself, I also attune to myself. A deeper connection, even though I am momentarily irrelevant.

Which is maybe the most counterintuitive answer to the question of who you are when you stop auditioning: you find out not by looking harder, but by looking somewhere else entirely.
